The American Nightmare

Human Trafficking Survivors' Fight for T-Visa

By Ashley Lee

Human trafficking is a form of modern-day slavery, where men, women, and children are coerced and defrauded into labor and commercial sex acts against their will. This brutal reality affects Americans, but often, it is foreign workers who watch their pursuit of the American Dream turn into a nightmare.

Who Are the Victims of Modern-Day Slavery?

Every dot represents a victim of human trafficking.

16,544 victims were identified in 2021 by the National Human Trafficking Hotline.

10,690 of them were women.

4708 were minors when they were trafficked.

2,754 were foreign nationals.

Anyone can become a victim of human trafficking. However, certain groups are at higher risk—those in need of shelter, people with a history of trauma, and immigrants without legal documentation in the U.S.

The statistics shown here come from nonprofits like the National Human Trafficking Hotline and Polaris, which identify and assist survivors.

But for survivors, the journey doesn’t end with finding help and escaping.

The Road to Recovery: A Difficult Path

After escaping trafficking, survivors often face significant obstacles in achieving financial and societal stability. For foreign workers, the first barrier is often immigration status.

Jean Stockdale, program director at Still Waters, a New Jersey-based nonprofit that assists foreign victims in securing immigration status, shared a chilling case of exploitation by Signal International, Inc.

Traffickers placed ads in newspapers across India and other countries, advertising jobs as welders and pipefitters with promises of a "Green Card opportunity." In search of a better life, workers borrowed money from local lenders to pay the traffickers' fees. What followed was forced labor under brutal conditions.

“They were kept in rooms with 12 bunk beds, and their passports were confiscated,” Stockdale explained. “They were guarded by armed security and a chain-link fence.”

Eventually, many victims contacted their families, leading to criminal and civil cases against Signal International.

Nonprofits like Still Waters helped survivors secure T-Visas, a special immigration status that allows certain trafficking victims to remain in the U.S. for up to four years. This has allowed many survivors to stay in the U.S. and rebuild their lives.

Yet, for many, this path is not so clear.

T-Visa: A Lifeline with Strings Attached

Each year, U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) can grant 5,000 T-Visas. However, only less than 2,000 are issued annually.

Among the cases that received a decision each year, the denial rate has steadily increased.

These numbers raise crucial questions about whether survivors truly have access to this vital protection. The T-Visa application process is complex and can involve multiple actors, leading to inconsistent outcomes. Many survivors, fearing deportation, choose not to apply at all.

The application process also requires cooperation with law enforcement—something that is often difficult for survivors due to concerns about arrest or deportation. Systemic racism and bias also influence how law enforcement handles trafficking cases, disproportionately affecting people of color.

Access to legal representation is crucial but often limited. Survivors in immigration detention face additional barriers, such as remote detention locations and fast-tracked court cases that prevent them from applying for the T-Visa.

While the T-Visa program offers protection for some survivors, it simultaneously puts many others at greater risk of deportation.

In 2018, USCIS began issuing Notice to Appear (NTA) to denied T-Visa applicants, a move that opens the door to deportation.

The shift in policy raises concerns about the safety of trafficking survivors, many of whom face increased risk of deportation.

While President Biden revoked the NTA policy in January 2021, with a second Trump administration potentially reintroducing harsher immigration policies, nonprofits like Still Waters are already feeling the impact of these shifting policies.

In 2020, the Trump administration suspended the U.S. Refugee Resettlement Program (USRAP) and withheld critical funding for refugee processing, freezing budgets for organizations like Still Waters. This suspension has left nonprofits struggling to support survivors and provide necessary services, including assistance with T-Visas.

Stockdale emphasized the uncertain future: “We don’t know how much longer we can keep going.”

Without resources and support, many survivors face the heartbreaking reality of a second trauma—one of legal battles and an uncertain future in a system that has already failed them once.
